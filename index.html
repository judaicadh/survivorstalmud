1948–1950 · Occupied Germany

The Survivors' Talmud

How a book printed by the surviving remnant of European Jewry was carried out of the ashes and scattered across the world.

Scroll to begin ↓

A world of learning, destroyed

For centuries, the great centers of Talmud study stood in the towns and cities of Central and Eastern Europe — in Vilna, Lublin, Warsaw, Frankfurt. The Shoah annihilated those communities. Yeshivot were emptied, libraries burned, and the printing houses that had produced the Talmud for generations were destroyed along with the people who read from them.

The surviving remnant

After liberation, hundreds of thousands of Jewish survivors gathered in the Displaced Persons camps of the American occupation zone. They called themselves the She'erit Hapletah — the surviving remnant. Amid the uncertainty of where they would live, they set about rebuilding a religious and cultural life: schools, newspapers, and study houses.

Printing the Talmud on German soil

Between 1948 and 1950, with the support of the United States Army, the surviving remnant printed a complete edition of the Babylonian Talmud — the first ever printed on German soil after the war. Its title page framed the volumes between two images: above, a view of the Land of Israel; below, a scene of barbed wire and a labor camp, inscribed with the words "from bondage to redemption, from darkness to great light."

Dispersion

The Survivors' Talmud was never meant to stay where it was made. As the camps closed and survivors emigrated — to Israel, to the United States, and beyond — they carried the volumes with them. Sets were given to libraries, congregations, and families, scattering the edition across the world.
